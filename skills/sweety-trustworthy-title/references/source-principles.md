# Source Principles

Source: `https://x.com/xionghuanwei/status/2063804329908412722`

The source argues that weak titles fail less because they are plain and more because they are performative: they pretend to have urgency, insider information, or unavoidable value that the body often cannot support.

Core extraction:

- A good title respects the reader before asking for a click.
- The title should make the object, action, and result clear.
- A small amount of suspense is acceptable only after the main subject and promise are clear.
- Title and body must stay strongly related; trust drops when the title raises emotion beyond what the body can deliver.
- Readers are increasingly sensitive to misleading framing, unverifiable sources, fictionalized inference, and hard emotional manipulation.

Seven anti-patterns to reject:

1. Shock framing: turns ordinary material into a disaster-level event.
2. Fear framing: stacks consequences before giving useful information.
3. Coercive framing: orders readers to read, save, share, or fear loss.
4. Suspended framing: hides subject and conclusion behind vague phrases.
5. Trend-jacking framing: borrows hot names or topics without information gain.
6. Over-promise framing: sells a complete outcome that the body cannot prove.
7. Conflict framing: inflates normal disagreement into camps or group war.

Recommended generation inputs:

- article topic
- core point
- target reader
- content type
- true support
- emphasis
- avoidance requirements

Recommended candidate angles:

- counter-intuitive
- pain point
- result
- truth
- comparison
- list
- emotion grounded in the content
